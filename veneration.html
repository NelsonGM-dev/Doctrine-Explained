🕯️ What is Veneration?

In Catholic theology, veneration (from Latin veneratio, meaning “respect” or “honor”) refers to:

Showing deep respect and honor—especially to saints, relics, or images—without giving them the full worship due only to God.

According to the Catholic Church:

  • Worship (Greek: latria) is given to God alone.
  • Veneration (Greek: dulia) is given to saints and their images.
  • Special veneration (hyperdulia) is given to Mary, above all saints, but still below God.

📌 Key point: Catholics say they do not worship statues, only “venerate” them as reminders or aids in devotion.

📖 But What Does the Bible Say?

The Bible doesn’t separate “worship” and “veneration” the way Catholic theology does. In God’s eyes, bowing, praying to, or trusting in a religious figure/image—regardless of intent—is a violation of His command.

🔥 Biblical Examples:

  • Exodus 20:4-5 – “You shall not make... any likeness... You shall not bow down to them or serve them.”
  • Isaiah 42:8 – “I will not give My glory to another or My praise to idols.”
  • Acts 10:25-26 – Cornelius bowed before Peter, but Peter said, “Stand up, I am only a man myself.”
  • Revelation 22:8-9 – John bowed to an angel, but the angel said, “Don’t do that! Worship God!”
💡 In each case, even acts of veneration were corrected or forbidden.

🎯 The Core Issue

Even if Catholics intend to venerate—not worship—saints or images:

🙏 The actions often mirror worship (kneeling, praying, trusting for help), and
📖 God never permits these practices in Scripture.

In the Bible, true worship is:

  • Spiritual (John 4:24)
  • Direct to God through Christ (1 Timothy 2:5)
  • Free of images and intermediaries (Deuteronomy 4:15-16)
